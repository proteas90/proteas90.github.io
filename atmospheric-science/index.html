Atmospheric Science

For being able to mitigate the negative consequences of natural hazards it is imperative to better understand their drivers. Floods, droughts, and extreme precipitation events are all hazards whose main drivers originate in the atmosphere. Being able to identify and quantify the connections of such hazards with atmospheric variables in different spatiotemporal scales can support Early Warning Systems.

I started diving into the atmospheric science in 2019, as an Early Stage Researcher within the EU funded Climate Advanced Forecasting of sub-seasonal Extremes (CAFE) project. My research focuses on the predictability of large-scale atmospheric flow patterns that are connected to extreme weather events in the Mediterranean. One of the aims of this work is to contribute to the development of operational products.

The work is currently in progress and some initial results are already available in a peer-reviewed publication (Mastrantonas et al, 2020). In this paper we have clustered the daily weather patterns over the Mediterranean to nine large-scale atmospheric flow regimes and quantified their connection to extreme precipitation events. These connections are promising for providing useful information about the probability of extreme precipitation events at sub-seasonal scales.
